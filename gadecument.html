Introduction & Motivation

Password security is a critical concern in modern computing systems. This project explores how evolutionary algorithms can be applied to simulate password cracking scenarios, helping us understand both the vulnerabilities of simple password systems and the power of nature-inspired optimization techniques.

Project Objectives

We investigate whether a Genetic Algorithm can efficiently discover a hidden 32-bit binary passcode through iterative evolution, mimicking the process of natural selection. The goal is to analyze convergence behavior, identify optimal parameters, and understand the relationship between genetic operators and solution quality.

This simulation provides insights into both cybersecurity challenges and the effectiveness of evolutionary computation for discrete optimization problems.

Genetic Algorithms: Nature-Inspired Computing

Genetic Algorithms are powerful optimization techniques inspired by the biological process of natural selection. They work by evolving a population of candidate solutions over multiple generations, where better solutions have higher chances of survival and reproduction.

Core Biological Analogy

Just as species evolve through genetic variation and natural selection, GAs evolve solutions through computational equivalents of genetic operations. The fittest individuals pass their genetic material to the next generation, gradually improving the population's overall quality.

Key Advantages

GAs excel at exploring large search spaces without requiring gradient information. They can escape local optima through mutation and maintain population diversity through crossover, making them particularly effective for complex discrete optimization problems like our passcode matching challenge.

Problem Formulation

We model the passcode cracking problem using genetic terminology that maps directly to biological evolution concepts.

Chromosome Representation

Each chromosome is a 32-bit binary string representing a candidate passcode. For example:

Target: 11010110100110011110001110101010
Candidate: 11010110100110011110001110101001

The candidate above differs by only 1 bit from the target.

Genetic Components

A gene is a single bit position (0 or 1) within the chromosome. The population consists of 200 chromosomes that evolve together over generations. Each generation applies selection pressure to favor chromosomes that more closely match the target passcode.

Fitness Function Design

The fitness function is the critical evaluation mechanism that guides the evolutionary process. It determines which solutions survive and reproduce.

Fitness(candidate) = Number of matching bits with target
Range: 0 (no match) to 32 (perfect match)

Evaluation Example

Target:    11010110100110011110001110101010
Candidate: 11010110100110011110001110101001

Fitness Score: 31 out of 32 (96.875% match)

This fitness function provides a clear gradient for the GA to follow. The closer a candidate gets to the target, the higher its fitness score and the more likely it is to pass its genetic material to the next generation.

Genetic Algorithm Implementation

Our implementation employs five essential genetic operators that work together to evolve high-quality solutions.

1

Selection

Tournament selection (k=3) chooses parents based on fitness competition

2

Crossover

Single-point crossover (80% rate) combines parent genetic material

3

Mutation

Bit-flip mutation introduces random variation to maintain diversity

Parameter Configuration Strategy

Successful GA performance depends critically on proper parameter tuning. We established baseline parameters through preliminary testing and then systematically varied the mutation rate to find optimal settings.

Parameter Value Rationale
Population Size 200 Balances diversity with computational efficiency
Crossover Rate 0.80 High rate promotes information exchange between solutions
Tournament Size 3 Moderate selection pressure prevents premature convergence
Max Generations 5000 Sufficient time for convergence on difficult instances
Mutation Rate Variable Primary tuning parameter: 0.005, 0.01, 0.02, 0.05

The mutation rate controls the exploration-exploitation tradeoff. Low rates may cause premature convergence, while high rates can disrupt good solutions. We tested four different values to identify the optimal balance.

Experimental Methodology

We conducted rigorous experiments to evaluate GA performance across different mutation rate configurations and ensure statistical reliability of our findings.

Experimental Design

For each of the four mutation rates (0.005, 0.01, 0.02, 0.05), we performed five independent trials, resulting in 20 total experimental runs. Each trial used a randomly generated 32-bit target passcode and tracked convergence behavior over time.

Data Collection

We measured three critical metrics for each run. The number of generations required to find the solution indicates convergence speed. The elapsed time in seconds provides practical performance insights. The convergence curve (best and average fitness per generation) reveals the evolutionary dynamics and stability of the search process.

All experimental data was systematically recorded in CSV format, with detailed convergence histories saved for post-analysis. This comprehensive data collection enables both quantitative performance comparison and qualitative understanding of evolutionary behavior under different parameter settings.

Experimental Results

Our experiments revealed clear patterns in how mutation rate affects GA performance. All configurations successfully found the target passcode, but with varying efficiency.

Analysis & Key Findings

üéØ Optimal Configuration Identified

The mutation rate of 0.005 consistently outperformed other configurations, achieving the fastest convergence with the highest stability across trials. This low mutation rate provided just enough genetic diversity to escape local optima while preserving beneficial genetic patterns discovered during evolution.

Impact of Mutation Rate

As mutation rate increased beyond 0.005, we observed progressively slower convergence. The mutation rate of 0.05 showed particularly erratic behavior, where excessive random changes disrupted promising solutions faster than crossover could propagate them through the population.

Theoretical Explanation

In our 32-bit problem, each mutation has a 1/32 probability of affecting a crucial bit position. At low mutation rates, beneficial mutations accumulate gradually. At high rates (0.05), approximately 1.6 bits flip per chromosome per generation, creating significant disruption that slows convergence despite maintaining high population diversity.

This demonstrates the classic exploration-exploitation dilemma in evolutionary algorithms. Our results suggest that for binary matching problems of this size, conservative mutation combined with effective crossover provides the optimal balance.

Conclusions & Implications

This project successfully demonstrated that Genetic Algorithms provide an effective approach to the passcode matching problem when properly configured. The systematic parameter tuning revealed that mutation rate has a profound impact on convergence behavior.
